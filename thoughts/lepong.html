pipeline · pixels in

canvas → ONNX → JEPA

Every 5 physics ticks (≈6 Hz) the client draws the Pong game on an offscreen 128x128 canvas whose JS render function is a line-by-line reimplementation of PongWorld.render() (world_model/envs/pong_world.py:155). If the occlusion slider above is non-zero, the client also blanks out a vertical strip on the right side of that canvas with fillRect before encoding. The frame is then passed directly to the ONNX runtime running in your browser — no server round-trip.

The model is a 13M-parameter CNN JEPA with a frozen encoder + predictor and a trained Linear(192, 10) state head. It receives 3 stacked frames as input and predicts normalized ball/paddle state. The paddle target comes exclusively from state_head(predictor(encoder(frames)))[ball_y]. Trainable parameters: 1,930 (just the state head). Frozen parameters: 13,082,080.

Sanity check: if the model stopped running, the paddle would freeze immediately (JEPA mode has no fallback).

partial observation · the lever

occlusion slider (0% / 20% / 40% / 60%)

Pong physics between bounces are trivial. Any algorithm that sees the ball plays near-optimally, which is why the v1 demo's "99% correlation" was a tautology. To force an actual prediction task, the occlusion slider blanks out a vertical strip on the right side of the canvas before it's encoded. At 0% the model sees the whole court. At 40% the model sees nothing on the right side and must imagine the ball whenever it crosses midfield.

You (the human) always see the whole court through the semi-transparent purple overlay and control the right paddle with the mouse. Only the encoder input is occluded — the display render is not.

This is an A/B dial: flip between 0% and 40% and watch whether the JEPA cyan ball still sits on top of the white real ball, and whether the median error in the live ball_y error panel climbs.

honest metrics

what we actually measure

Running p50 / p95 / max absolute error of JEPA's prediction and the classical baseline vs ground truth, separately for ball_y and ball_x. All computation happens in-browser — no server logging.

The classical baseline is NOT a fair comparison — it has direct access to ground-truth state and integrates physics (walls + no paddle lookahead) for 5 steps from that state. JEPA has only the pixel bytes. When classical wins on median it's because it's an oracle, not because the baseline is smart. The interesting questions are: (a) how close does JEPA get despite having less information? (b) does JEPA's tail (p95, max) degrade gracefully or catastrophically? (c) does occlusion break JEPA?

We do not cite the win-rate score against a human mouse opponent as a research result — any paddle that reliably intercepts the ball wins 60:1 against a casual human. The score is a playability sanity check, not a benchmark.

TL;DR

A 13M-parameter JEPA (CNN encoder + 6-layer transformer predictor + linear state head) plays Pong from raw pixels, running entirely in your browser via ONNX. It receives 128x128 frames, predicts ball position through a trained state head, and drives the left paddle. No server, no simulator, no game state — just pixels in, paddle target out.

The occlusion slider is the interesting part: at 0% the model sees everything and the task is trivial. At 40% the right side of the court is blacked out before the model sees it, forcing genuine prediction under partial observability. The live error panels show whether the JEPA or a classical physics extrapolator (which cheats by reading ground-truth state) does better.

Full research journal, training recipes, and the obliteration post-mortem of the v1 demo are at github.com/SotoAlt/aura.
