how silent actually works

What is JEPA? Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture — Yann LeCun's framework for world models that predict in a compact latent space instead of generating pixels. Fast enough for real-time planning, small enough to train in hours on one GPU.

This experiment: the JEPA is the blind echolocating predator. Its only sensor is a 4-channel cardioid mel-spectrogram — what its ears hear from where it is in the room, every tick. You're the human player (the teal dot). You move, you make footsteps, you can leak audio if you stand still too long. The predator pings, listens to its own echoes plus your sounds, and runs CEM in the JEPA's latent space to plan its next move toward where it thinks you are.

No simulator on the model's side. No vision. Pure audio in, latent rollout out, action picked by cost in latent space. The hunt is the model.

the four player roles

Every JEPA demo we ship has the same shape: the JEPA is the agent executing the canonical loop (encode → predict → CEM → act). If the JEPA is the agent, the human's job has to be exactly one of: (1) define the goal, (2) be the environment, (3) be the target, or (4) be the adversary. Anything else (JEPA as helper, copilot, UI widget) isn't a JEPA demo — it's a sidebar.

RELAY is role 4 (adversary — same task, separate turns). Silent is role 3: you are what the JEPA is trying to catch.

the stack

Silent follows the LeWM paper byte-for-byte: a 6M-param ViT-Tiny encoder trained from scratch (with a 4-channel input convolution instead of 3 — that's the only architectural change for audio), a 6-layer AR transformer predictor, two losses — next-embedding MSE plus SIGReg. On top of that we add a DexWM-style joint state head trained alongside the predictor at λ=10 — this is what lets the planner reason about real positions in the room instead of raw latent geometry.

Audio observation: 4 cardioid microphones at the predator's position, pointed N/E/S/W. Each one captures a 50-frame mel-spectrogram of what it hears: the predator's own pings, the player's footsteps, the player's idle "breathing" leak when standing still, and (early phases) a beacon hum at the exit. Stacked into a 4×64×50 tensor, bilinearly upsampled to 224×224, fed to the encoder.

the planner

Every tick: encode the last 3 audio observations (stride 5 env-steps apart). Run CEM in latent space — sample 24 candidate action sequences from a Gaussian, pass each through the predictor once, decode each predicted final embedding through the post-hoc state head to get a predicted (predator_xy, player_xy). Score each candidate by predicted predator-to-player distance. Take the top elites, refit, iterate twice. Apply the best first action. Repeat.

Cost is measured in decoded state space, not raw latent distance. That's because the planner doesn't care if two embeddings are similar — it cares if its predicted action gets it closer to the prey. The post-hoc head is what makes that cost meaningful.

the beacon was a lie

For three weeks the model used a beacon — a constant low hum at the exit door — as an ambient reference signal. The thinking: a JEPA encoder needs some persistent audio anchor to learn self-localization, otherwise the latent space drifts.

Then we tried removing it (Phase 3E). No beacon, no ambient, just predator pings + player audio. The encoder collapsed to its training spawn-region prior — every match the predator drifted to the bottom-right corner of the map. Confirmed. We had a load-bearing shortcut.

Except the next step was to randomize starting positions in the data. With nothing to anchor on AND varied spawn points, the encoder had to learn self-localization the hard way — by attending to the actual geometry of the audio (footsteps, voice, ping echoes). It worked. Phase 3E ep30 hunts cleanly across all rooms, no spawn-region collapse, no beacon needed.

Lesson: a "free" supervised signal can be a shortcut that hurts task performance. Removing the apparent crutch forced the encoder to learn the actually-useful representation. We'd seen this twice before in the project — fixing it for the third time finally made us stop trusting probe metrics blind.

why our metrics kept lying

Probe R² (how well a linear regression decodes ground-truth state from the encoder's embedding) disagreed with gameplay quality three times on this project: once on RELAY v9, twice on Silent. Different models, same trap.

incidentprobe saysgameplay says
RELAY v9val_state 0.007 ("great")predictor under-predicts motion 60–85%
Silent 3Dbetter fits than baselinehunts worse than baseline
Silent 3Eworse fits than 3Dhunts better than every prior model

The structural mismatch: probe R² measures whether you can recover absolute coordinates from the embedding — an unconditional encoder property. The CEM planner doesn't care about absolute coordinates; it cares about relative geometry under counterfactual actions — does this candidate thrust shrink the predicted predator-to-player vector? That's a conditional predictor property. The two correlate in the easy regime and decorrelate exactly where models actually differ.

Conclusion: probe R² is a coarse health check, not a model-comparison metric. Behavior-side benchmarks (headless gameplay win-rate, CEM rank-correlation against pymunk ground truth) are the real ship gate. We'd been letting an unconditional metric arbitrate a conditional decision.
