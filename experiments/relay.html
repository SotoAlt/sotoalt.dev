[relay] a JEPA plans against you in latent space

A 26M-param JEPA opponent in a turn-based T-block puzzle. You push the T into your goal. The opponent thinks in pure embedding space — no physics simulator at inference.
model
ViT-Tiny encoder + 6-layer AR transformer
params
26M
planning
CEM in latent space

what's happening

Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) is Yann LeCun's framework for world models that predict in a compact latent space instead of generating pixels. Fast enough for real-time planning, small enough to train in hours on one GPU.

The puzzle: two balls, a T-shaped block, two goals on opposite sides of the board. You (amber) push the T toward your goal — the opponent (cyan) pushes toward its own. 5 turns each, 5 environment steps per turn. Whoever has the T closer to their goal at match-end wins. Classical pymunk physics simulates collisions for execution; the model is only ever used for planning.

What the JEPA does each turn: encode the last 3 frames of pixels into a 192-dim latent, sample 96 candidate action sequences, predict each one's terminal embedding through the autoregressive transformer, decode each predicted state back to (x, y) positions via a small MLP head, score on ‖agent − T‖ + 2·‖T − goal‖. Top 15% = elites, refit Gaussian, repeat 6 iterations. Best plan executes. No simulator on the model's side.

why "retrained" matters

The first version of this model failed in a specific, instructive way: its training data alternated which ball was acting on every single frame, but real gameplay has each ball act for a full 5-frame turn at a time. The temporal structure didn't match, and the model never cleanly learned which thrust belonged to which ball. Cross-agent leakage on every prediction.

The fix was a one-line change in the data generator (who = 'p1' if ((t // 5) % 2 == 0) else 'p2') plus retraining the position decoder on uniformly-random scenes (Silent's recipe — frozen encoder + post-hoc head trained on uniform samples instead of rollout-distribution samples). Position decode error dropped from ~45 px to ~10 px on agents, and average planning error dropped from 54 px to 47 px on the calibration probe. Lesson: in turn-based games, training data has to match the temporal structure of gameplay.

the stack

Architecture follows the LeWorldModel paper byte-for-byte: a 6M-param ViT-Tiny encoder trained from scratch, a 6-layer AR transformer predictor with AdaLN modulation, two losses — next-embedding MSE plus SIGReg (Sketch Isotropic Gaussian Regularizer, the trick that prevents embedding collapse without any of the usual stabilization knobs). On top: a DexWM-style joint state head (small MLP: 192 → 256 → 256 → 8) trained alongside the predictor — it's what lets the planner reason about real-world distances instead of raw latent geometry.

Training data: 12,500 episodes × 40 steps × 5 regimes (contact_push 40%, approach_no_push 25%, null_thrust 15%, near_t 10%, far_from_t 10%). 500K frames at 224×224 RGB. ~10 epochs from scratch on one RTX 4090, ~$3 / 75 minutes.

what relay proves

Each demo in this series stacks toward a bigger thesis. lepong showed JEPA can track a ball from occluded pixels. relay shows JEPA can plan strategically against a human opponent in turn-based puzzle mechanics — opponent takes ~2 seconds per turn on CPU, no GPU needed at inference. Everything runs on a $14/month Hetzner box.
